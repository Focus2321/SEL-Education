Supporting Social & Emotional Learning in the Classroom

As educators, we play a crucial role in shaping children's social and emotional development. The skills children develop today will influence their relationships, academic success, and overall well-being throughout their lives.

Early Influences on Social/Emotional Growth

The foundation for social and emotional development begins before birth and continues rapidly through early childhood. Prenatal factors such as maternal stress, nutrition, and exposure to toxins can influence a child's temperament and emotion-regulation capabilities.

During the first three years of life, children form attachment relationships that serve as templates for future social interactions. Secure attachments foster trust, empathy, and emotional security, while adverse experiences can disrupt healthy development.

These early experiences create neural pathways that affect how children respond to stress, form relationships, and regulate emotions throughout adolescence.

Developmental Stages and Milestones

Infancy (0-2 years)

Key Social/Emotional Milestones:

  • Forms attachment bonds with primary caregivers
  • Develops basic trust and security
  • Begins to express and recognize basic emotions

Classroom Strategies:

  • Maintain consistent caregiving routines to build security and trust
  • Use “serve and return” interactions—responding to babbling, gestures, and cries

Early Childhood (3-5 years)

Key Social/Emotional Milestones:

  • Develops sense of self and independence
  • Begins cooperative play and sharing
  • Expresses more complex emotions and early empathy

Classroom Strategies:

  • Implement emotion-labeling activities and feelings charts
  • Create structured opportunities for cooperative play and problem-solving

Middle Childhood (6-11 years)

Key Social/Emotional Milestones:

  • Develops more complex friendships and peer relationships
  • Gains awareness of social rules and expectations
  • Develops more sophisticated emotion-regulation strategies

Classroom Strategies:

  • Implement collaborative projects that require negotiation and compromise
  • Teach conflict-resolution skills through role-playing and guided practice

Self-Regulation in the Classroom

Self-regulation refers to a child's ability to understand and manage their emotions, thoughts, and behaviors in different situations. It includes the capacity to calm down when upset, focus attention, adjust to changes, and control impulses. Strong self-regulation skills are linked to better academic performance, healthier relationships, and improved mental-health outcomes.

Classroom Examples

Breathing Exercises

Teach children simple breathing techniques like “balloon breathing” (deep belly breaths) or “five-finger breathing” (tracing fingers while breathing) to use when feeling overwhelmed or excited.

Calm-Down Corner

Create a designated space with comfort items, emotion cards, and sensory tools where children can go to regain emotional control before rejoining group activities.

Emotion Thermometer

Use visual scales to help children identify their emotional state and choose appropriate regulation strategies based on their “temperature.”

How Teachers Can Model Self-Regulation

  • Managing Frustration: Verbalize your own calm-down strategies when faced with challenges (“I'm feeling frustrated right now, so I'm going to take three deep breaths before we continue.”)
  • Mindfulness Breaks: Lead brief mindfulness or stretching sessions to reset focus during transitions.
  • Emotion Vocabulary: Frequently name and validate feelings (“It sounds like you're disappointed because recess ended.”)

Family Strategies for Supporting SEL

For Infants (0-2 years)

  • Establish consistent daily routines for feeding, sleeping, and play
  • Respond promptly and warmly to cries and communication attempts
  • Narrate your actions and label emotions during daily interactions

For Early Childhood (3-5 years)

  • Create emotion check-in rituals using simple charts or pictures
  • Read books featuring characters experiencing different emotions
  • Practice turn-taking games and sharing activities at home

For Middle Childhood (6-11 years)

  • Hold family meetings to practise problem-solving and conflict resolution
  • Create a “feelings vocabulary” word wall to expand emotional literacy
  • Role-play challenging social scenarios and brainstorm responses together
